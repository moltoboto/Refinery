Neuralink's Progress Is Real — But the Superintelligence Promise Is Still Distant

Neuralink has reached a milestone that matters: patients in its early human trials can now control a computer cursor using brain signals. For people with severe paralysis, that is a meaningful clinical outcome. But the company continues to carry a much larger public narrative — one that points toward AI-linked cognitive augmentation and "superhuman" capability — and the distance between those two things is still significant. Brain signals are noisy, variable, and highly individual. Scaling from cursor control to broad high-level interaction is not a software iteration problem; it is a long-horizon neuroscience and medical engineering challenge. Near-term BCI progress is real and medically important. The superintelligence-era vision remains a long-horizon hypothesis, not a near-term product roadmap. Read more →

OpenAI Rotates macOS Signing Certificates After Supply-Chain Security Incident

OpenAI disclosed that a compromised version of the Axios developer library was pulled into a GitHub Actions workflow used in its macOS app-signing process. The company found no evidence of data exposure or software tampering but is rotating certificates and requiring users to update ChatGPT Desktop, Codex, and Atlas. Older builds stop receiving updates from May 8. Read more →

Digital-Twin AI Study Links Loneliness and Insomnia to Substantially Higher Type 2 Diabetes Risk

Researchers at Anglia Ruskin University modeled data from 19,774 UK adults over up to 17 years and found that loneliness, insomnia, and poor mental health each correlated with an estimated 35-point rise in diabetes risk and 78 points when all three appeared together. Because the model requires no bloodwork or wearables, researchers say it could support affordable early screening at population scale. Read more →

Tesla's FSD Supervised Clears First European Regulatory Gate in the Netherlands

Dutch road authority RDW authorized FSD Supervised after 18 months of testing, making the Netherlands the first EU country to allow the system on public roads. Drivers remain legally responsible — this is supervised assistance, not autonomous operation — but the approval gives Tesla a compliance reference it can carry into other EU market conversations.
